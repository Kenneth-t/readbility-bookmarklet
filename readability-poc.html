How EU policy is made

The European Union only takes action in those areas where it has been explicitly authorised to do so.

During regular summits, national leaders and the Commission President set the EU's overall political direction. For 2014 to 2019, President Jean-Claude Juncker has set 10 political priorities for the Commission, on which it bases its work.

To ensure that EU action is effective, the Commission assesses the impact of policies, legislation, trade agreements and other measures at every stage - from planning to implementation and review. This is called smart regulation.

STEP 1

Planning

Before the commission starts a new initiative it:

  • describes it in a roadmap
  • examines potential economic, social and environmental consequences with an impact assessment
  • consults with the public and stakeholders, for example via public consultations.

Roadmaps describe the problems a planned Commission policy should address, ways to implement it (new law, recommendation to EU countries, etc.) and the different steps of development, including stakeholder consultation and the impact assessment.

Impact assessments describe the need for EU action and the implications of policy choices. They may find that no action should be taken at EU level. Assessments are also examined by an independent board.

Public consultations enable businesses, private individuals and organisations to express their views on initiatives at various stages of the process. Groups of outside experts can also be called on for advice.

STEP 2

Putting policy into practice

Once a policy has been through this process, the Commission can implement it through one or more of the following:

  • laws
  • recommendations and opinions
  • funding programmes
  • international agreements.

Laws

The Commission can draft new EU laws and propose them to the European Parliament and the Council of the EU, which represents the governments of the 28 EU countries. Both can then amend the draft and have to adopt the law before it enters into force (ordinary legislative procedure).

Recommendations and opinions

These are non-binding and:

  • help address national or EU-wide issues
  • suggest a line of action for EU countries
  • support the preparation of legislation in EU countries
  • provide guidance on the interpretation and content of EU law.

Funding

The Commission makes direct financial contributions from the EU budget. Grants are given to projects or organisations that contribute to the implementation of an EU programme or policy.

Executive agencies help the Commission manage EU funding programmes.

International agreements

The Commission speaks on behalf of all EU countries in international bodies like the World Trade Organisation.

It also negotiates international agreements for the EU such as the Transatlantic Trade and Investment Partnership (TTIP) with the United States.

STEP 3

Applying EU law

EU law is applied in EU countries through:

  • Directives
  • Regulations

Directives set a goal for all EU countries. It is up to the individual countries how to achieve it.This often requires national governments to change their existing laws or adopt new laws.

Regulations are immediately and directly applicable in all EU countries. If further explanation is needed on how those rules should be implemented - for instance very technical details - the European Parliament and national governments can give the Commission the power to adopt implementing acts (formerly known as comitology procedure).

STEP 4

Enforcing EU law

The Commission checks that EU law is applied properly.

EU countries face formal infringement procedures and can be referred to the European Court of Justice, if they

  • fail to incorporate EU directives into their national law
  • don't report to the Commission which measures they have taken
  • are suspected of breaching EU law.

If companies breach EU competition laws - for instance by forming cartels - the Commission investigates the case and can impose fines.

Submit a complaint

If you think an EU country is not correctly applying EU law, you can:

  • complain to the commission
  • submit a petition to the European Parliament

STEP 5

Evaluating EU policies

The Commission regularly checks the performance, purpose and financing of initiatives such as laws, trade agreements and funding programmes.

Evaluations are planned several years in advance. The results are fed into the Commission work programme for the upcoming year.

Part of this is the regulatory fitness and performance programme (REFIT). It aims to make EU law simpler and reduce regulatory costs.
